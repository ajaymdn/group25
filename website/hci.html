Human Computer Interaction (HCI)

This research concerned the interface for the original MotionInput app-library that was proposed.

Introduction

To gain a deeper understanding of users' needs and concerns, we conducted two semi-structured interviews [5], one with a Star Wars fan and gamer who we feel represents the average user in our project, and another with an individual who suffers from mild multiple sclerosis, to get a better understanding of the accessibility concerns users may have. The interviews were insightful and encouraged us to conduct further research on, for instance, the Star Wars Connect Motion game as well as assistive gaming tech like the Xbox adaptive controller mentioned in the interviews. Defining the Problem

After brainstorming using our personas and interviews, we identified three key user needs our project needed to solve to reach the greatest audience alongside how we would achieve this.

  1. Immersion - a more realistic gaming experience compared to traditional gaming methods. Achieved through more interactive aspects of motion and voice. â€‹
  2. Accessibility â€“ provide a new format for previously disenfranchised users, i.e., those unable to use traditional controllers. Achieved through voice commands, prioritisation of more general motion over use of fine hand motion.â€‹â€‹
  3. Affordability â€“ a new experience for low-cost requirement for equipment. Achieved through need for video camera alone.â€‹ â€‹
The focus was placed on implementing this gaming format in RPG and FPS games due to their already immersive nature of playing as a sole character.â€‹

Our identification of the three user needs came from our research of the user base. For instance, a small survey conducted to determine peopleâ€™s interest in motion gaming [6] indicated that the majority of people will engage with it if it is â€˜worthwhileâ€™. Immersion is therefore essential to distinguish a motion gaming experience from a traditional one. Another study on gaming device ownership [7] indicated that 46% of gamers use a laptop, and so affordability and accessibility are important to enable users to play with the devices they already have.â€‹

Once we determined the needs of our userbase we began to consider more carefully the implementation. To identify a game to base our first implementation on we researched all the Star Wars games available and categorized them based on the mode and style of gameplay, format, etc. Consequently, we decided to focus on The Knights of The Old Republic game as it is an RPG and includes key elements such as combat and other gestures that one would want to execute through motion input. Studying this gave us a better idea of how to go about sketching for our first prototype.â€‹

Sketches

To start off we created a series of sketches [8] which targeted the idea of movement, look-around and a menu system. For movement we experimented with immersion and accessibility; where we went with the use of hand movements/ leg movements. Another alternative was the use of on-screen buttons where the user can place their hand/fist, which results in movement (As if they were using arrow keys). However , based on user feedback, we concluded that using on-screen buttons defeated the concept of immersion and thought it would be better to implement visual movement of the legs/arms. As for the menu, we produced three iterations, but with the idea of accessibility we wanted to go with a menu that uses less buttons to reduce clutter on the GUI and go with four options of the menu. â€‹

Prototype

To produce the actual prototype [9], we decided to design it using Figma software, which allowed it to be more interactive. We created a prototype of the in-game movement and look-around system. We decided to use real gestures for movement, as immersion was identified as one of the needs of our users. Next, we recorded ourselves simulating several different gestures to change the direction of the camera, until we ended up with the one described in our prototype (dragging the camera with the active hand). We showed the set-up settings and the motion prototype to our potential users, which provided us with the opportunity to get feedback.â€‹ â€‹

In the second part of our work on prototypes, we focused on capturing the important functionality of menu control using motion/voice input (we decided to include both control styles for greater accessibility). These images show how we envisioned the radial menu implementation (which we chose as the most appropriate for menu design based on feedback). We also provide detailed instructions on how the user should navigate in our prototype, which are used to give users a better idea of how the real game behaves. Finally, one of our team members recorded themselves on camera and "interacted" with the menu prototype to validate the appropriateness of our design.â€‹
